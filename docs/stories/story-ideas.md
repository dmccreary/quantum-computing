# Graphic Novel Story Ideas

Ten short graphic novel stories (12–14 panels each) exploring how cognitive
bias shapes decisions in quantum computing — not as a tale of villains, but
as a human portrait of how brilliant, well-meaning people can be fooled by
their own minds.

Each story is anonymized. Characters are composites, not real individuals.

---

## Story 1: The Slide That Launched a Thousand Grants

**Bias:** Availability heuristic / Narrative fallacy
**Protagonist:** Dr. Priya — a brilliant theoretical physicist
**Setting:** A conference keynote, then her university lab

**Premise:** Dr. Priya gives a dazzling 20-minute talk showing a simulation
where a quantum circuit outperforms a classical one on a toy problem. The
slides are beautiful. The audience is electrified. Grant applications flood
in from researchers who weren't in the room but saw the slide deck online.
Nobody notices — including Priya — that the "classical baseline" in the
benchmark was a slow, unoptimized algorithm from a 1998 textbook.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Priya at the whiteboard — elegant equations, genuine excitement
- Panel 2: Conference hall, standing ovation, camera flashes
- Panel 3: The slide in question — "100× speedup" in bold red
- Panel 4: A grad student in the back row squints at the fine print
- Panel 5: Priya's inbox explodes with collaboration requests overnight
- Panel 6: A journalist screenshots the slide — "Quantum Breakthrough!"
- Panel 7: Three other labs launch copycat experiments based on the talk
- Panel 8: Six months later — the grad student runs the classical baseline properly
- Panel 9: The gap closes. Then disappears. Then reverses.
- Panel 10: Priya stares at the new results in silence
- Panel 11: She rewrites the paper. The correction gets 12 citations. The original: 4,000.
- Panel 12: Final panel — Priya teaching a class: "Always benchmark against the best classical algorithm."

**Thematic note:** No fraud. Just the seductive power of a compelling story
and a striking number. The vivid image (the slide) became more real than
the underlying math.

## Story 2: The Sunk Cost Superconductor

**Bias:** Sunk cost fallacy
**Protagonist:** Marcus — engineering director at a quantum hardware startup
**Setting:** A startup office, 2019–2024

**Premise:** Marcus's team has spent five years and $80 million building a
superconducting qubit system. Error rates are stuck at 0.3% — ten times
higher than what fault-tolerant computing requires. Every quarter, the board
asks: "Are we close?" Every quarter, Marcus says: "We just need one more
breakthrough." He genuinely believes it. The money already spent feels like
evidence that the goal must be reachable.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: 2019 — Marcus at a whiteboard, team of 12, full of energy
- Panel 2: First milestone — 5 qubits working. Champagne.
- Panel 3: The error rate graph — flat for 18 months
- Panel 4: Board meeting — "We've invested too much to stop now"
- Panel 5: Marcus restructures the team. Doubles down on one approach.
- Panel 6: A competitor pivots to a completely different architecture
- Panel 7: Marcus dismisses it: "They're starting over. We have a head start."
- Panel 8: 2023 — error rates still flat. Half the team has quietly left.
- Panel 9: A new engineer asks the question nobody asked in year one: "What would it take to admit this approach won't work?"
- Panel 10: Stunned silence in the room
- Panel 11: Marcus alone, late at night, finally reading the competitor's paper
- Panel 12: Final panel — Marcus at a new company, year one, with a whiteboard that says: "What's our exit criterion?"

**Thematic note:** Sunk cost isn't greed — it's the painful human difficulty
of letting go. Marcus is sympathetic throughout.

## Story 3: The Journalist's Deadline

**Bias:** Anchoring bias / Authority bias
**Protagonist:** Sasha — a technology journalist
**Setting:** A newsroom and a quantum lab, over 48 hours

**Premise:** A quantum company sends Sasha an embargoed press release: "First
quantum computer to solve a problem impossible for classical machines." She
has 48 hours to publish. The CEO is charming and quotable. The press release
has impressive graphs. She files the story. Two days later, three computer
scientists post a preprint showing a laptop solved the same problem in 4
seconds.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Sasha's editor drops the press release on her desk: "This is huge — file by Thursday"
- Panel 2: The CEO demo — slick, rehearsed, optimistic
- Panel 3: Sasha's notes: "First ever… impossible for classical… historic"
- Panel 4: She calls one academic. He says "sounds impressive." She stops there.
- Panel 5: Headline goes live: "Quantum Leap: The Day Classical Computing Lost"
- Panel 6: Her story is shared 40,000 times in 12 hours
- Panel 7: A preprint drops. Three researchers, a laptop, 4 seconds.
- Panel 8: Sasha reads it on her phone, color draining from her face
- Panel 9: The correction is published. 300 shares.
- Panel 10: A reader emails: "Why didn't you ask a skeptic?"
- Panel 11: Sasha's new process — a Post-it on her monitor: "Who benefits from this claim being true?"
- Panel 12: Final panel — Sasha at a conference, asking the hard question nobody else is asking

**Thematic note:** Deadline pressure and authority bias aren't excuses, but
they are explanations. Sasha learns — and the lesson is actionable.

## Story 4: The Professor's Popular Book

**Bias:** Overconfidence bias / Optimism bias
**Protagonist:** Professor Eli — a quantum physicist turned science communicator
**Setting:** Book tour, then his university office, three years later

**Premise:** Eli writes an accessible book for general audiences predicting
that quantum computers will solve climate change, cure cancer, and break all
encryption within a decade. It's a bestseller. He's not lying — he genuinely
believes it. But simplifying for a popular audience meant dropping all the
caveats. By the time the decade is up, none of the predictions have arrived,
and policymakers made real decisions based on his book.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Eli writing at his desk, genuinely excited, coffee going cold
- Panel 2: Publisher feedback: "Great! Can we make the timeline more concrete? Readers love that."
- Panel 3: The book cover — bold, colorful, "The Quantum Decade"
- Panel 4: Morning talk show — "Professor, when will quantum change everything?"
- Panel 5: A senator's aide reading the book on a plane, flagging pages
- Panel 6: A national quantum initiative is launched, partly citing the book
- Panel 7: Five years later — a journalist asks Eli about the predictions
- Panel 8: "Well, it's more complicated than I may have suggested…"
- Panel 9: The senator's aide, now a policy director, asks: "When do we get results?"
- Panel 10: Eli reads a new paper — the timeline has slipped another decade
- Panel 11: He writes a follow-up piece: "What I got wrong, and why"
- Panel 12: Final panel — the follow-up piece has 1/10 the readers of the book

**Thematic note:** Optimism bias in public communication has downstream
consequences. Eli is not a fraud — he's a victim of his own enthusiasm.

## Story 5: The Investor Who Couldn't Say No

**Bias:** FOMO / Social proof
**Protagonist:** Valentina — a venture capital partner
**Setting:** Silicon Valley, a conference, a fund pitch, 2021

**Premise:** Valentina passes on a quantum startup in 2020. Six months later
it raises a massive Series B. She hears about it at a conference from three
different people in one day. By the afternoon she's reconsidering. By evening
she's calling the founder. She invests at a 10× higher valuation than she
could have. The technology hasn't changed at all — only the social signal has.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: 2020 — Valentina reads the pitch deck. "Interesting but too early." Pass.
- Panel 2: Six months later — headline: "QuantumX raises $200M Series B"
- Panel 3: Conference lobby — first mention. She nods politely.
- Panel 4: Conference lunch — second mention. She starts to feel uneasy.
- Panel 5: Conference cocktail hour — third mention. "Everyone's talking about it."
- Panel 6: She pulls up the pitch deck again. Same deck. Same technology.
- Panel 7: "Why does it feel different now?"
- Panel 8: She calls her analyst: "Run the numbers again."
- Panel 9: Analyst: "Nothing has changed technically, Valentina."
- Panel 10: She invests anyway. FOMO wins.
- Panel 11: Two years later — the startup pivots to "quantum-inspired classical" software
- Panel 12: Valentina at a whiteboard with her team: "What changed between the pass and the check?"
- Panel 13: Answer on the whiteboard: "Social signal. Not evidence."

**Thematic note:** FOMO is an ancient survival instinct misfiring in a
modern market. Valentina is a smart, experienced investor — that's the point.

## Story 6: The Halo Effect in the Lab

**Bias:** Halo effect / Authority bias
**Protagonist:** Dr. Chen — a Nobel Prize-winning physicist who pivots to quantum computing
**Setting:** A new quantum research institute, year one

**Premise:** Dr. Chen won the Nobel for work in condensed matter physics —
genuinely brilliant work. A tech company funds a new quantum institute and
asks her to lead it. Her reputation attracts top students and press coverage.
But quantum computing requires a completely different skill set. Her intuitions
from condensed matter don't transfer cleanly. Nobody tells her this because
nobody wants to question a Nobel laureate.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Dr. Chen on stage receiving her Nobel — genuine triumph
- Panel 2: The company CEO approaches her backstage with a proposal
- Panel 3: New institute — ribbon cutting, cameras, smiling faces
- Panel 4: Her first technical meeting — she proposes an approach from her old field
- Panel 5: Junior researchers exchange glances but say nothing
- Panel 6: A postdoc quietly runs simulations. The approach won't scale.
- Panel 7: The postdoc drafts a memo — then deletes it. "Who am I to say?"
- Panel 8: A year passes. The approach is at a dead end.
- Panel 9: Dr. Chen finds the postdoc's deleted memo in a shared drive backup
- Panel 10: "Why didn't you send this?"
- Panel 11: "I didn't think it was my place."
- Panel 12: Dr. Chen calls a full team meeting: "From now on, I need you to challenge me."
- Panel 13: Final panel — the postdoc presenting a critique, Dr. Chen taking notes, smiling

**Thematic note:** The halo effect harms the person being idealized as much
as it harms those who stay silent around them.

## Story 7: The Quantum Winter Is Coming

**Bias:** Normalcy bias / Escalation of commitment
**Protagonist:** Director Amara — head of a national quantum program
**Setting:** A government research agency, over four years

**Premise:** Amara manages a $500M national quantum initiative. After three
years, the technical progress is real but modest — far slower than the
original projections that got the program funded. Each annual review, she
presents the best results, emphasizes what worked, minimizes what didn't.
Not dishonestly — just optimistically. The program continues. The gap between
public statements and internal reports quietly widens.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Program launch — press conference, handshakes, ambitious timeline on a banner
- Panel 2: Year 1 review — results are promising. Report is accurate.
- Panel 3: Year 2 review — results are mixed. Report is "cautiously optimistic."
- Panel 4: A program officer flags the gap between projections and actuals
- Panel 5: Amara: "We don't want to spook the funding committee."
- Panel 6: Year 3 — a competitor nation announces a "breakthrough" (unverified)
- Panel 7: Amara uses it to justify continued funding: "We can't fall behind."
- Panel 8: Internal report vs. public statement — side by side. Growing divergence.
- Panel 9: A journalist files a FOIA request for the internal reports
- Panel 10: The gap becomes public. Not fraud — just optimism compounded over time.
- Panel 11: Amara testifies before a committee: "We believed the projections were achievable."
- Panel 12: Final panel — new program charter on her desk: "Annual honest assessment, published publicly."

**Thematic note:** Institutional optimism bias is how programs drift from
evidence without anyone making a single dishonest decision.

## Story 8: The Quantum-Inspired Rebrand

**Bias:** Framing effect / Ambiguity effect
**Protagonist:** CEO Jordan — founder of a classical optimization software company
**Setting:** A startup, a marketing meeting, an investor conference

**Premise:** Jordan's company makes excellent classical optimization software
for logistics. It works. It sells. But "classical" doesn't attract headlines.
A consultant suggests: "What if we called it quantum-inspired?" The algorithms
don't change. The results don't change. But the pitch deck gets a quantum
circuit diagram on the cover, and suddenly investors who ignored them for
years are taking meetings.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Jordan's product — genuinely good software, modest growth, solid customers
- Panel 2: Marketing consultant presents rebranding options
- Panel 3: "Quantum-inspired optimization." The room lights up.
- Panel 4: Jordan: "But we don't use any quantum hardware…"
- Panel 5: Consultant: "Inspired by. The algorithms draw on quantum principles." (Vague enough.)
- Panel 6: New pitch deck — quantum circuit diagram, blue glow, same product inside
- Panel 7: Investor conference — suddenly three VCs want meetings
- Panel 8: A technical investor asks: "Walk me through the quantum component."
- Panel 9: Jordan's explanation is technically defensible but deliberately vague
- Panel 10: Funding closes. The team feels uneasy. The product hasn't changed.
- Panel 11: A customer asks: "Will this work better on a real quantum computer someday?"
- Panel 12: Jordan pauses. Thinks. Then: "Let's talk about what your actual problem is."
- Panel 13: Final panel — new company tagline: "Advanced optimization. No buzzwords required."

**Thematic note:** The framing effect makes identical things feel different.
Jordan isn't lying — but the label creates expectations the product can't meet.

## Story 9: The Grad Student Who Asked the Wrong Question

**Bias:** Conformity bias / In-group bias
**Protagonist:** Tomás — a first-year PhD student in quantum information
**Setting:** A top university quantum research group

**Premise:** Tomás reads the group's most celebrated paper — the one that
put the lab on the map — and notices something odd in the methodology. He
raises it quietly with a labmate. The labmate warns him off: "That paper
is why we all have funding." Tomás nearly drops it. Instead he runs the
numbers carefully and writes a polite, rigorous replication attempt. It
doesn't replicate. He has to decide: publish or bury it.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Tomás arrives at the lab — excited, first day, photos on the wall of famous alumni
- Panel 2: He reads the landmark paper. Makes notes. Frowns.
- Panel 3: Asks a labmate: "Did anyone check the noise model in section 4?"
- Panel 4: Labmate goes quiet. "That paper is why we all have funding."
- Panel 5: Tomás closes his notebook. Goes back to assigned work.
- Panel 6: Three weeks later — he can't stop thinking about it
- Panel 7: He runs a careful replication. Takes two months.
- Panel 8: Results don't match. The effect shrinks to noise.
- Panel 9: He shows his advisor. Long silence.
- Panel 10: Advisor: "You could be wrong." Tomás: "I know. I've checked six times."
- Panel 11: They submit a replication note. Reviewers push back hard.
- Panel 12: It publishes. The original authors respond professionally. The field updates.
- Panel 13: Final panel — Tomás teaching a seminar: "The hardest question to ask is the one that challenges work everyone wants to be true."

**Thematic note:** Conformity bias operates most strongly in tight-knit
communities with shared stakes. Tomás's courage is quiet, not dramatic.

## Story 10: The CEO's Five-Year Forecast

**Bias:** Planning fallacy / Optimism bias
**Protagonist:** CEO Riley — founder of a quantum computing startup
**Setting:** A company launch, investor roadshow, and five years later

**Premise:** At launch, Riley confidently forecasts: fault-tolerant quantum
computing in 5 years, commercial revenue in 3. The forecast is sincere.
It's based on a best-case extrapolation of current progress — the kind
every founder makes. The investors know this but fund it anyway. Five years
later, the company is doing real work but is nowhere near the forecast.
Nobody lied. The planning fallacy did what it always does.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Riley on stage at launch — confident, roadmap on the screen
- Panel 2: The roadmap — Year 1: 50 qubits. Year 3: revenue. Year 5: fault tolerance.
- Panel 3: Investor Q&A — "Are these timelines realistic?" Riley: "Conservative, actually."
- Panel 4: Year 1 — 50 qubits achieved. On track! Team celebration.
- Panel 5: Year 2 — error rates not improving as fast as modeled. First slip.
- Panel 6: Revised roadmap — quietly issued. New Year 5 = original Year 3.
- Panel 7: Year 3 — no revenue. "We're in investment mode."
- Panel 8: A board member pulls out the original roadmap. Side-by-side with today's.
- Panel 9: Riley: "The underlying science took longer than we projected."
- Panel 10: Board member: "You projected the best case. You always do."
- Panel 11: New planning process — three scenarios: optimistic, realistic, pessimistic
- Panel 12: Riley presenting the pessimistic scenario publicly for the first time
- Panel 13: An investor afterward: "That's the most honest pitch I've seen in this industry."
- Panel 14: Final panel — the pessimistic scenario is still more optimistic than reality. That's the planning fallacy.

**Thematic note:** The planning fallacy is universal and well-documented.
Riley is doing exactly what every human does when forecasting complex
technical work — and the consequences compound at scale.

## Story 11: The Lecture That Started Everything ⭐

**Tone:** Celebratory, warm, inspirational — the one origin story
**Protagonist:** Richard Feynman — physicist, Nobel laureate, incorrigible
curiosity engine
**Setting:** MIT, May 1981 — the First Conference on the Physics of Computation

**Premise:** Richard Feynman is already one of the most celebrated physicists
alive when he takes the stage at a small MIT conference on the physics of
computation. He hasn't prepared a polished talk. He has a question that has
been nagging him — something nobody in the room has quite put into words yet.
What if you can't simulate quantum mechanics on a classical computer no matter
how big it gets? And if that's true… what would a computer built from quantum
mechanics itself be able to do? In one afternoon, in front of a few dozen
people, he sketches the idea that will become an entire field.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Feynman at his Caltech office the night before — not preparing slides, just scribbling on a napkin, brow furrowed, clearly chasing something
- Panel 2: MIT campus, spring morning — a small conference room, maybe 40 people, folding chairs, overhead projector. Not a stadium. Just people who care.
- Panel 3: Feynman at the board before his talk, chatting with a grad student, laughing about something unrelated — completely at ease
- Panel 4: He begins without preamble. "I want to talk about simulating physics." Marker in hand, tie loose.
- Panel 5: He draws a simple grid on the board — a classical simulation of particles. "To simulate N quantum particles, you need exponentially more memory. The universe doesn't do that. Why does our computer have to?"
- Panel 6: Close-up on faces in the audience — some confused, some leaning forward, one person's pen hovering over paper, not writing, just listening
- Panel 7: Feynman grins. "Nature isn't classical, dammit. And if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical." The room stirs.
- Panel 8: He sketches something new on the board — a rudimentary circuit made of quantum elements. "What if the computer itself obeys quantum rules?"
- Panel 9: A hand goes up: "But how would you control it?" Feynman: "I don't know. That's the fun part." Laughter. But he means it.
- Panel 10: After the talk — a cluster of people around the board, pointing, arguing, excited. Feynman in the middle, sleeves rolled up, loving every second.
- Panel 11: A young researcher writes in her notebook: "Universal quantum simulator — is this possible?" She will spend the next 20 years finding out.
- Panel 12: Years pass — montage panel: papers published, labs launched, companies founded, flags planted on technical milestones — all tracing back to one afternoon and one question
- Panel 13: Final panel — a modern quantum lab, humming dilution refrigerators, engineers in cleanroom suits — and on the wall, a single quote: *"Nature isn't classical, dammit."* — R. Feynman, 1981

**Thematic note:** This is the story's heart. No bias, no cautionary lesson —
just the pure ignition of a genuine idea. Feynman's gift was not certainty
but *curiosity without ego*: he didn't claim to have the answer, he just
couldn't stop asking the question. Every field needs a moment like this.
Place this story first, before the ten cautionary tales, so readers understand
what real scientific excitement looks like — and why it is worth protecting
from the hype that followed.

## Story 12: The Best Wrong Turn

**Tone:** Honest, ultimately hopeful — a coming-of-age story about finding
your real value
**Protagonist:** Zara — a sharp, hardworking undergraduate physics major
**Setting:** A university, a job market, and a quantum sensing startup, over
eight years

**Premise:** Zara is a college junior when her professor — charismatic,
well-funded, genuinely enthusiastic — convinces her that quantum computing
is the most important field of the century and that PhDs will be in
extraordinary demand. She believes him. She spends five years earning her
PhD in quantum error correction. Then she graduates into a job market where
every quantum computing opening wants five years of industry experience and
her competition is a hundred other PhDs from better-funded labs. She sends
out sixty applications. She gets four interviews. She gets zero offers.
But the story doesn't end there. The skills she built — deep comfort with
linear algebra, noise modeling, precision measurement, cryogenic systems,
and relentless debugging of systems that almost work — turn out to be
exactly what a quantum sensing startup desperately needs. Quantum sensing
is real, it is growing, and it is hiring. Zara didn't end up where she
planned. She ended up somewhere better.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Zara as a junior, sitting in the front row of her professor's quantum computing lecture — wide-eyed, taking furious notes, completely hooked
- Panel 2: After class — the professor tells her: "The PhDs who graduate in this field in five years are going to have their pick of jobs. The industry is exploding." He means it sincerely.
- Panel 3: Zara calls her parents that night: "I'm going to get my PhD in quantum computing." Her dad: "Will there be jobs?" Zara: "Professor says absolutely."
- Panel 4: Five years — a montage of the PhD: late nights, whiteboards covered in stabilizer codes, a cryostat that won't behave, a thesis defense that goes well, a handshake and a diploma
- Panel 5: The job search begins. Zara's spreadsheet: 60 applications, color-coded. The "quantum computing" column fills with polite rejections.
- Panel 6: Rejection email close-up: "…looking for candidates with 5+ years industry experience in fault-tolerant systems…" She has none. Almost nobody does.
- Panel 7: She meets a friend from her cohort at a coffee shop. He got a job — at a classical HPC company, using his optimization skills. "It's not what I pictured," he says. "But it's real."
- Panel 8: Zara updates her search. She types "quantum" into a job board and changes the filter. Quantum sensing. Quantum networking. Quantum metrology. A different list appears.
- Panel 9: She reads about quantum sensing — atomic clocks precise enough to detect gravitational shifts underground, magnetometers that can map brain activity without touching the skull, navigation systems that work without GPS. Real products. Real customers. Real revenue.
- Panel 10: Interview at a quantum sensing startup — the hiring manager slides a whiteboard marker across the table: "Walk me through noise floor modeling in a two-level system." Zara smiles. She could do this in her sleep.
- Panel 11: Offer letter. She reads it twice. The salary is higher than any quantum computing role she interviewed for.
- Panel 12: First week — Zara at a bench covered in optics hardware, a colleague explaining the application: a handheld magnetometer for medical imaging that costs 1/100th of an MRI machine. "We're shipping 200 units next quarter."
- Panel 13: Six months in — Zara presents her noise reduction work to the team. A colleague says: "Where did you learn to think about errors this carefully?" Zara laughs: "Quantum error correction. Turns out it's useful even when you're not correcting quantum computers."
- Panel 14: Final panel — Zara back at her old university, guest-lecturing to undergrads. On the whiteboard behind her: *"The skills are real. The applications are wider than you think."* A student in the front row is taking furious notes.

**Thematic note:** This story carries a message the book needs to deliver
carefully: studying quantum computing is not a mistake, even if quantum
computing itself never achieves commercial viability. The mathematics of
quantum mechanics — linear algebra, Hilbert spaces, density matrices,
noise modeling — is genuinely hard and genuinely useful. The engineering
disciplines it demands — cryogenics, photonics, precision fabrication,
low-noise electronics — are in demand across quantum sensing, quantum
networking, defense, medical imaging, and fundamental research. The problem
is not the education. The problem is the professor's forecast, made with
confidence he had no basis for. Zara's story is not a tragedy. It is a
lesson in the difference between a field's *skills* and a field's *hype* —
and a reminder that the map of valuable careers is much larger than any
single technology promise.

**Skills highlighted in the story:**

| Skill developed in QC PhD | Where it transfers |
|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Noise and error modeling | Quantum sensing, signal processing, RF engineering |
| Linear algebra and Hilbert spaces | Machine learning, optimization, simulation |
| Cryogenic systems | Quantum sensing, space systems, particle physics |
| Precision measurement | Metrology, medical devices, navigation |
| Debugging near-impossible systems | Any deep-tech engineering role |
| Rigorous uncertainty quantification | Finance, climate modeling, drug development |

## Story 13: The Decoherence Clock

**Bias:** Optimism bias / Illusion of control
**Protagonist:** Dr. Yuki — an experimental physicist specializing in superconducting qubit hardware
**Setting:** A university quantum hardware lab, over six years

**Premise:** Yuki has dedicated her career to extending qubit coherence times —
the microseconds a qubit can hold its quantum state before the environment
destroys it. Each year she publishes an improvement. Each year the press
release says the same thing: "Record coherence time achieved." Each year
she believes the next breakthrough will clear the bar required for
fault-tolerant computing. What she slowly realizes — and struggles to accept
— is that every technique that extends coherence in one dimension introduces
a new error source in another. The problem is not a wall she can climb over.
It is a horizon that moves.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Yuki as a postdoc, celebrating a result: coherence time up from 50 to 100 microseconds. A genuine milestone. Champagne in paper cups.
- Panel 2: Her grant proposal, year one: "Projected coherence time in five years: 10 milliseconds. Required for fault tolerance: ~1 millisecond. We will get there."
- Panel 3: The lab — a forest of cables descending into a dilution refrigerator humming at 15 millikelvin. Beautiful, alien hardware.
- Panel 4: Year two — new shielding technique works. Coherence up to 500 microseconds. Press release written. Optimism high.
- Panel 5: But the new shielding changes the electromagnetic environment. A different error mode appears. Coherence in a new axis drops.
- Panel 6: Yuki at her desk late at night, staring at a graph. The total error rate hasn't changed. She's been trading one problem for another.
- Panel 7: Conference — another lab announces 1.2 milliseconds coherence. Everyone is excited. Yuki asks quietly: "What's their two-qubit gate fidelity?" Silence.
- Panel 8: The dirty secret of the field, spelled out on her whiteboard: coherence time × gate fidelity × qubit count = a product that hasn't meaningfully improved in three years despite dozens of "record" announcements.
- Panel 9: She writes a review paper titled "Are We Making Progress?" — carefully framed, heavily cited, genuinely uncertain in its conclusion.
- Panel 10: Reviewers push back. "This is too negative." One reviewer: "You're not accounting for future techniques we haven't invented yet."
- Panel 11: Yuki revises. Softens. The paper publishes. The honest version lives in her drawer.
- Panel 12: A younger colleague reads the published version and asks: "Do you actually think we'll get there?" Long pause.
- Panel 13: Yuki: "I think we might. But I used to be certain. Now I just think we might."
- Panel 14: Final panel — her lab notebook, open to a page of coherence measurements across six years. The curve is real progress. And it is not enough. Both things are true.

**Thematic note:** This story lives in the physics, not the hype. Yuki is
not fooled by press releases — she writes them. The illusion of control
is subtler here: the belief that sustained experimental effort must
eventually converge on the answer, because it always has in physics before.
Decoherence may be different. The horizon may genuinely move. That is the
hardest thing for a scientist trained on solvable problems to internalize.
Pairs directly with Chapter 5 (Physics Barriers and Hardware Platforms).

## Story 14: The CISO Who Spent the Budget Early

**Bias:** Availability heuristic / Manufactured urgency
**Protagonist:** Nadia — Chief Information Security Officer at a large financial institution
**Setting:** A corporate boardroom, an IT infrastructure project, and a quiet reckoning, over four years

**Premise:** Nadia reads a headline: "Quantum Computers Will Break All
Encryption Within Five Years." It is 2021. The article quotes a government
official, two professors, and a quantum computing company that sells
"quantum-safe" products. Nadia takes it to her board. The board authorizes
$2M to replace the company's public-key cryptography infrastructure
immediately. The work is painful, expensive, and disruptive. It finishes
in 2023. In 2024, NIST finalizes its post-quantum cryptography standards —
the ones her new system isn't fully compatible with. And in 2025, no quantum
computer capable of breaking RSA-2048 exists, or is close to existing. She
spent the budget solving a problem that was a decade away, using standards
that weren't ready yet, guided by urgency manufactured partly by vendors
who benefited from her fear.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Nadia at her desk, coffee in hand, reading the headline: "Quantum Threat to Banking Encryption: Experts Say Clock Is Ticking"
- Panel 2: She pulls the article apart — the "five years" claim traces back to one quantum company's investor presentation, not a peer-reviewed source
- Panel 3: But the board meeting is tomorrow. The article is from a major publication. She puts her notes away and prepares the slide deck.
- Panel 4: Board meeting — the CTO asks: "How certain is this timeline?" Nadia: "Uncertain. But the downside risk is catastrophic if we wait."
- Panel 5: $2M approved. The infrastructure project begins.
- Panel 6: Eighteen months of pain — legacy systems, vendor negotiations, staff retraining, two failed migrations rolled back
- Panel 7: A vendor calls: "Our quantum-safe solution is fully certified." (NIST hasn't finalized standards yet. "Certified" by whom?)
- Panel 8: Project completes. Nadia presents to the board: "We are quantum-safe." Applause.
- Panel 9: Three months later — NIST releases final post-quantum standards. They differ from what her vendor implemented.
- Panel 10: A security researcher emails her: "Your new system uses a non-standard lattice variant. NIST went a different direction."
- Panel 11: Nadia stares at the email. Then pulls up the original headline that started everything. Traces the "five years" claim to its source for the first time.
- Panel 12: The source: a slide in a Series B pitch deck from a quantum computing startup. The startup has since pivoted.
- Panel 13: New policy document on her desk: "Threat Timeline Verification Protocol — all urgency claims must be traced to primary sources before budget authorization."
- Panel 14: Final panel — Nadia speaking at a security conference: "The quantum threat is real. The timeline was manufactured. Know the difference."

**Thematic note:** Manufactured urgency is one of the most effective sales
techniques in deep-tech: create fear, compress the timeline, sell the
solution before the standards exist. Nadia is a skilled professional who
did her job responsibly by the norms of her field. The failure was
upstream — in how technical claims travel from a pitch deck to a
boardroom without their uncertainty attached. Pairs with Chapter 6
(Can Quantum Computers Break Encryption?) and Chapter 4 (Catalog of
Optimistic Claims).

## Story 15: The Career Incentive Loop

**Bias:** Systemic incentive misalignment / No single villain
**Protagonists:** Five characters whose individually rational decisions
collectively sustain a field that none of them would fund if starting from scratch
**Setting:** Interleaved — a university, a VC firm, a newsroom, a government
agency, and a startup — same year, different rooms

**Premise:** This story has no single protagonist. It follows five people
on the same day, showing how each makes a completely rational career
decision that — when combined with the others' decisions — locks the
quantum computing hype cycle in place. Nobody chose this outcome. The
system produced it. This is the hardest kind of problem to fix because
there is no villain to confront.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1 (split — five small scenes): Five people wake up on the same morning. A grad student. A professor. A VC partner. A journalist. A program officer at a federal agency.
- Panel 2 — The Grad Student: Choosing a thesis topic. Quantum computing gets three times more funding than her first-choice topic in classical optimization. She pivots. Rational.
- Panel 3 — The Professor: Writing a grant proposal. The program officer told him last year that "quantum" keywords doubled the review score. He adds a quantum angle to work that doesn't need one. Rational.
- Panel 4 — The VC: Reviewing a portfolio. Three of his competitors have quantum investments. His LP asks why he doesn't. He schedules a meeting with a quantum startup. Rational.
- Panel 5 — The Journalist: Pitching stories. Her editor rejected two classical computing pieces this month. The quantum piece ran on the front page. She pitches another quantum angle. Rational.
- Panel 6 — The Program Officer: Defending her agency's budget. Congress wants to know if America is keeping up with China's quantum investment. She recommends increasing funding. Rational.
- Panel 7: Midday — all five make their decisions. The grad student files her thesis proposal. The professor submits the grant. The VC takes the meeting. The journalist files the story. The program officer writes the memo.
- Panel 8: Three years later — montage. The grad student graduates. The professor gets funded. The VC invests. The story runs. The program is renewed.
- Panel 9: A researcher from outside the field reads all five outcomes in a single afternoon. She draws a diagram — arrows connecting each decision to the next, a closed loop with no entry point for doubt.
- Panel 10: She labels it: "The Career Incentive Loop. Input: one optimistic claim. Output: five years of self-sustaining momentum. No fraud required."
- Panel 11: She presents it at a workshop. The room is quiet. Someone says: "So how do you break it?"
- Panel 12: She points to the diagram. "You'd need all five to change at once. Or you'd need one of them to have a very bad day and tell the truth publicly."
- Panel 13: Final panel — the same five people, five years later. The grad student is doing quantum sensing (she loves it). The professor got a second grant. The VC wrote down the position. The journalist won an award for a corrective piece. The program officer is quietly revising the success metrics. Change is possible. It is just slow.

**Thematic note:** This is the systems thinking story — the one that
shows how a feedback loop operates without bad actors. Each individual
is sympathetic. The loop is the problem. Students who feel frustrated
by the persistence of hype despite abundant skeptical evidence will
find the explanation here: no single intervention point, no single
moment of dishonesty, just five rational people optimizing for their
own careers inside a system that rewards optimism and penalizes doubt.
Pairs directly with Chapter 13 (Systems Thinking and Causal Loop
Diagrams), specifically the "Career Incentive Loop" section.

## Story 16: We've Seen This Movie Before

**Bias:** Pattern blindness / Failure to use reference class forecasting
**Protagonist:** Constance — a veteran technology investor in her late 50s
**Setting:** A technology conference in the present day, intercut with
memories spanning 30 years

**Premise:** Constance has been investing in deep technology since the
early 1990s. She was in the room for cold fusion. She covered the Segway
launch. She sat through the fuel cell decade. She watched ITER fusion
reactor timelines slip by twenty years. She's now at a quantum computing
conference, and she keeps experiencing a strange déjà vu — not because
she's seen quantum before, but because she's seen *this*: the language,
the narrative arc, the promises, the timeline, the way journalists ask
questions, the way founders dodge them. She knows this story. She's just
never been able to make anyone else see it in time.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Present day — Constance at a quantum computing conference, badge around her neck, coffee in hand, watching a keynote with a familiar expression
- Panel 2: The keynote screen: "Fault-tolerant quantum computing within reach — transformational applications by 2030." Constance's expression doesn't change.
- Panel 3: Memory flash — 1989. A conference room. "Cold fusion confirmed — limitless clean energy within reach." Same font weight. Same podium energy.
- Panel 4: Back to present — a journalist asks the founder: "What's the biggest remaining challenge?" Founder: "Really just an engineering problem at this point."
- Panel 5: Memory flash — 2001. A Segway launch event. A founder: "This will change how cities are built. The engineering is solved."
- Panel 6: Back to present — slide on screen: "Timeline to commercial deployment: 5–7 years." Constance writes something in her notebook.
- Panel 7: Her notebook, revealed: a table. Technology. Promised timeline. Actual timeline. Cold fusion: abandoned. Fuel cells: 30+ years and counting. ITER: slipped 25 years. Segway: discontinued.
- Panel 8: Coffee break — a young analyst approaches her: "What do you think? Is this the real thing?" Constance: "What reference class are you using?"
- Panel 9: Analyst, confused: "Reference class?" Constance: "How often do technologies that look like this, at this stage, actually deliver on this timeline?"
- Panel 10: She shows him the notebook. He studies it. "But quantum is different — it has real physics behind it." Constance: "They all had real physics behind them."
- Panel 11: Afternoon panel — a skeptic on stage. The audience grows restless. Someone mutters: "Why is she so negative?" Constance thinks: *She's not negative. She's done the reference class.*
- Panel 12: End of conference — Constance walks out into the sunlight. She doesn't know if quantum will work. She genuinely doesn't. But she knows what a field looks like when it's inside the hype bubble, and she knows what it looks like five years after the pop.
- Panel 13: She calls her analyst: "Pass. Not because the physics is wrong. Because the timeline is borrowed from a different movie, and we've seen how it ends."
- Panel 14: Final panel — her notebook, open to a new page, heading: "Quantum Computing." The table has one row started. Column headers: *Technology. Promised. Actual. Notes.*

**Thematic note:** Reference class forecasting is one of the most powerful
and most ignored tools in technology assessment. Constance's superpower
is not cynicism — she would invest if the evidence warranted it. Her
superpower is memory: she has personally lived through enough hype cycles
to recognize the narrative pattern before the outcome is known. This story
teaches students that history is not just context — it is data. Pairs with
Chapter 15 (Historical Parallels and Lessons) and Chapter 10
(Technology Assessment).

## Story 17: The Road Not Funded

**Bias:** Opportunity cost blindness / Availability bias
**Protagonist:** Dr. Imogen — a computer scientist working on classical
algorithms for combinatorial optimization
**Setting:** A university, a grant review panel, and a biotech company,
over five years

**Premise:** Imogen has spent a decade developing a new class of classical
algorithms for protein structure prediction — genuinely hard computational
problems that quantum proponents claim will require quantum computers to
solve. She applies for federal funding three times. Each time, the review
panel notes that "quantum approaches are expected to outperform classical
methods in this domain within a decade" and directs funding toward quantum
teams instead. Imogen keeps working on a shoestring. In year four, her
algorithm matches AlphaFold on a benchmark. In year five, a biotech company
licenses it and uses it to identify a drug candidate that goes to trial.
Nobody headlines this as a win for classical computing. The quantum grants
that displaced her funding produce three papers and no products.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Imogen at her whiteboard — dense graph theory, combinatorial structures, a genuine research problem that has consumed her for years
- Panel 2: Grant application, round one. She describes the algorithm's potential. Reviewer comment: "Promising, but quantum annealing approaches are expected to surpass classical methods here. Recommend redirecting."
- Panel 3: She reads the comment twice. Pulls up the paper the reviewer cited. The quantum "surpassing" is a theoretical projection on a toy problem with 20 variables. Her real problems have 10,000.
- Panel 4: She applies again. Different framing. Same result. The word "quantum" appears four times in the rejection letter.
- Panel 5: Her lab shrinks. One postdoc leaves for a quantum startup. She runs the experiments herself on nights and weekends.
- Panel 6: A quantum computing team at another university, funded at 10× her budget, publishes results on the same problem class. Their best result: 50 variables. Runtime: 2 hours.
- Panel 7: Imogen's algorithm on the same benchmark: 50 variables. Runtime: 4 seconds. She almost doesn't bother publishing it.
- Panel 8: She publishes. A blogger picks it up: "Classical algorithm beats quantum on standard benchmark." It gets 800 retweets.
- Panel 9: A biotech company emails her. They've been waiting for exactly this capability. Can she license it?
- Panel 10: License signed. The biotech team runs her algorithm on a real drug discovery problem — 8,000 variables, real biology, urgent timeline.
- Panel 11: Eighteen months later — a drug candidate identified. Pre-clinical trials begin. Classical compute time: 11 hours on a laptop cluster.
- Panel 12: Imogen reads the quantum team's latest paper. Title: "Toward quantum advantage in combinatorial optimization: progress and remaining challenges." Their benchmark: 200 variables.
- Panel 13: She is not bitter. She is just tired. She writes an op-ed: "Every dollar chasing quantum advantage in this domain is a dollar not spent on algorithms that work today."
- Panel 14: Final panel — a grant review panel, five years later. A reviewer reads Imogen's op-ed aloud. The room is quiet. Someone adds a new criterion to the review rubric: "Classical baseline: what is the best available non-quantum approach, and has this been compared?"

**Thematic note:** Opportunity cost is the invisible casualty of hype.
Nobody chose to underfund Imogen — they chose to fund quantum, which
seemed more promising. The damage is in the counterfactual: the drug
candidates not found, the algorithms not built, the years not spent on
approaches that actually worked. This story does not argue that quantum
research should stop. It argues that every investment has an alternative
use, and that hype distorts the comparison. Pairs with Chapter 14
(Better Alternatives) and Chapter 8 (Investment Risk Analysis).

## Story 18: The Quantum Speedup That Wasn't

**Bias:** Complexity theater / Illusion of understanding
**Protagonist:** Professor Darius — a quantum algorithms theorist, and
Maya — a science journalist who covers his work
**Setting:** A university, a newsroom, and a computer science conference,
over eighteen months

**Premise:** Darius publishes a paper showing an exponential quantum speedup
for a certain class of optimization problems. It is careful, peer-reviewed
work. The speedup is real — asymptotically. Maya covers it for a major
publication: "Quantum Algorithm Delivers Exponential Speedup Over Classical
Computers." The headline is technically defensible. What neither Darius's
paper nor Maya's article explains clearly is that the speedup is over the
*previous best known* classical algorithm, that a better classical algorithm
might exist and hasn't been found yet, and that "exponential speedup" on
problems with inputs of size 10 still means the quantum computer finishes
in a millisecond versus a nanosecond — slower in practice because of
overhead. Eighteen months later, a classical algorithms team publishes a
new method that closes the gap entirely. The quantum speedup evaporates.

**Key panels:**
- Panel 1: Darius at his desk, finishing the paper. He is careful. The claims are technically accurate. He is proud of the work.
- Panel 2: The paper publishes. A university press office asks: "Can we put out a release?" Darius: "Sure, but be careful about how you frame the speedup."
- Panel 3: Press release headline: "Exponential Quantum Speedup Demonstrated for Key Optimization Problem." Darius reads it. Winces slightly. Lets it go.
- Panel 4: Maya reads the press release and the abstract. The abstract is dense. The press release is clear. She uses the press release.
- Panel 5: Her article: "Quantum Algorithm Leaves Classical Computers in the Dust." It is shared 60,000 times. Darius's inbox fills up.
- Panel 6: A classical algorithms researcher reads the article over breakfast. She pulls up the paper. Starts reading footnote 7. Something catches her eye.
- Panel 7: The footnote: "The classical lower bound assumes the best-known algorithm. Improved classical methods may reduce or eliminate this gap."
- Panel 8: She emails her grad students: "New project. Find a better classical algorithm for this problem."
- Panel 9: Six months of work — whiteboards, dead ends, a breakthrough at 11pm, a paper drafted
- Panel 10: Classical paper publishes: "Matching Quantum Performance on [Problem Class] with a New Randomized Algorithm." Runtime gap: eliminated.
- Panel 11: Darius reads it. His reaction is professional and genuine: "This is good work. This is how science is supposed to work."
- Panel 12: Maya is asked to write a follow-up. Her editor: "Is this a big deal?" Maya: "It means the original speedup claim no longer holds." Editor: "How many people read that original article?" Maya: "Sixty thousand." Editor: "How many will read the correction?" Both know the answer.
- Panel 13: The correction runs. 900 readers. The original article still shows up first in search results with its original headline intact.
- Panel 14: Final panel — Maya and Darius at a panel together: "What should I have said differently?" Darius: "Speedup over best known classical. Those four words change everything." Maya writes it down.

**Thematic note:** The gap between "exponential speedup" and "exponential
speedup over the best-known classical algorithm, pending further classical
research" is technically small and practically enormous. This story
illustrates why complexity claims require careful reading — not because
researchers are dishonest, but because the norms of technical writing
are not the norms of public communication, and nobody owns the translation
layer between them. The story also demonstrates the self-correcting nature
of science working as intended — and the asymmetry in how corrections
travel compared to original claims. Pairs with Chapter 2 (Quantum
Algorithms and Limits) and Chapter 3 (History of Quantum Computing).
